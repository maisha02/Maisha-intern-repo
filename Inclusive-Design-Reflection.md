# Inclusive Design Reflection

## Vulnerable Populations
Vulnerable populations include people with ADHD, Autism, or other cognitive challenges. In digital spaces, they may struggle with focus, overload, unclear instructions, or frustration when tasks feel too complex.

## Ethical Considerations
It is important to design in a way that does not overwhelm users. This includes using simple layouts, clear instructions, and avoiding unnecessary complexity. Respecting different needs and experiences is key.

## Accessibility for ADHD and Autism
Using simple language, clear steps, and predictable layouts helps reduce cognitive load. Breaking tasks into smaller steps and avoiding too many distractions can make tools easier to use.

## Supporting Team Members
We can support neurodivergent teammates by being clear in communication and flexible with working styles. Giving structured tasks and allowing time to process information can help.

## Reflection

## Inclusive Communication
I can make my communication more inclusive by being clear, direct, and avoiding unnecessary complexity. I should also be patient and open to questions.

## Common Pitfalls
Too much information at once, unclear instructions, and cluttered design can make tools hard to use. Lack of structure can also cause confusion.

## Practical Change
One change I can make is breaking tasks into smaller, clear steps making it easier for them to understand. This will make work easier to follow and less overwhelming.

## Task

## First-Person Insight
As someone with AHDH and reading about it, many users struggle with starting tasks and staying focused. Tools that feel overwhelming or unclear can makes this feeling worse.

## Design Improvement
A helpful improvement would be showing one step at a time instead of everything at once. This can reduce overwhelm and improve focus.

## Supportive Response
I would probably say something like "I understand that getting started can feel difficult sometimes. Try focusing on just one small step first, and take breaks if needed. You’re making progress even with small efforts, and that’s what matters."
